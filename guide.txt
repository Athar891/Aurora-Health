1. Goals Onboarding 
The goal-setting flow when you first sign up is a strong feature. We want to see this retained and 
polished in the final build. 
 
2. End-of-Day Report 
The app should automatically send users a daily end-of-day summary report — a recap of their 
progress, goals hit, and what is still pending. This should feel personalized and motivating. 
 
3. Hydration Quick-Add 
The quick-add interaction seen in the hydration feature is the right UX pattern for habit tracking. 
This style of frictionless, one-tap logging should be extended across other goals throughout the 
app. 
 
4. Skip Buttons with Gentle Nudges 
Add skip buttons throughout the onboarding setup, but pair them with encouraging prompts that 
remind users to complete their setup when they are ready to start working on a goal. For 
example: "You can finish setting up when you are ready to start your first goal." 
 
5. Tagline Rewording 
The current opening line "Your AI Companion" should be updated to something warmer and 
more wellness-focused. Suggestions: "Your Health Buddy" or "Your Wellness Partner." The goal 
is for it to feel personal, not clinical. 
 
6. Anova Speaks in the Intro 
In the intro and onboarding screens, have Anova itself speak — introducing the app in first 
person. This gives the brand personality and makes the first impression feel alive and human.

Additional Feature Insights & Best Practices 
--- 
 
7. Streaks with Grace Days 
Streaks are a powerful motivator but need a grace day or streak freeze so that missing one day 
does not cause people to quit entirely. Losing a long streak is one of the top reasons users 
abandon wellness apps. 
 
8. Frictionless Minimum Actions 
Make the smallest possible action the default — so easy it feels almost too simple. This follows 
the 2-minute rule: the lower the barrier to logging or completing a habit, the more consistently 
people do it. The hydration quick-add is a good example of this done right. 
 
9. Adaptive Goals 
If someone is consistently hitting 100% of their goal, the app should nudge them to level up. If 
they are struggling, it should suggest scaling back without judgment. Goals that never change 
feel stale; goals that adjust feel alive. 



 
10. Personalized Onboarding That Learns Your Lifestyle 
Onboarding should ask about sleep schedule, work hours, and energy levels so that reminders 
and recommendations feel tailored, not generic. Apps that feel built for you specifically see 
much higher long-term retention. 
 
11. Progress Visibility Over Perfection 
Showing someone they are 70% of the way there is more motivating than showing they missed 
30%. Frame progress positively. The language and framing around incomplete goals is critical 
— it should never feel like a punishment. 
 
12. Identity-Based Framing 
Outcome-based goals ("I want to drink more water") are less effective than identity-based ones 
("I am someone who stays hydrated"). Where possible, the app should reinforce who the user is 
becoming, not just what they are tracking. 
 
13. Celebrate Small Wins 
A subtle animation, a short encouraging line, or a simple acknowledgment after completing a 
goal goes a long way. These micro-moments of celebration are what make an app feel 
rewarding rather than transactional. 
 
14. Weekly Reviews 
A weekly summary showing patterns over 7 days gives users perspective they cannot get from 
daily numbers alone. The best version asks one simple reflection question rather than a long 
survey. 
 
15. Smart Notifications 
Notification fatigue is one of the top reasons people delete wellness apps. Reminders should 
feel timely and personal — ideally let users pick their own reminder times based on their routine. 
Contextual nudges (reminding someone to hydrate after logging a workout) feel intelligent rather 
than automated. 
 
16. Light Social and Accountability Features 
Even minimal social features — sharing a streak with a friend or having one accountability 
partner — significantly improve retention. Public commitments are more powerful than private 
ones. Leaderboards should be optional since they motivate some users and demotivate others. 
 
17. Cross-Goal Insights 
Connecting dots between goals is one of the most powerful things a wellness app can do. For 
example: "On days you sleep 7 or more hours, you hit your movement goal 80% of the time." 
This kind of insight makes the data feel meaningful rather than just informational. 
 
18. Re-Engagement Strategy 
Most wellness apps optimize for the day-one experience and forget about week three, which is 



when the majority of users fall off. A strong re-engagement approach — knowing what to do 
when someone goes quiet for 3 days — is what separates apps that retain users from ones that 
get deleted. This is the single biggest differentiator for long-term success.
